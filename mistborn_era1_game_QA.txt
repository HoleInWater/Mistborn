================================================================================
  SECTION 1 — PHYSICAL METALS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  IRON  |  Allomancy: Ironpulling  |  Feruchemy: Weight  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Physical Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What exactly does burning iron allow a Lurcher to Pull, and what can they NOT Pull?

A: Burning iron lets a Lurcher Pull on metal objects — coins, weapons, armor, anything
   made of or containing metal. They cannot Pull on living flesh or organic matter. The
   metal must exist as actual metal. This is why Allomancers can be partially countered
   by simply not carrying metal on your person.

GAME: How will your engine distinguish "metal" from "non-metal" objects in the world?
      Consider a tagging or material system on game objects. What happens when a player
      tries to Pull on a wooden sword with a metal pommel — does only the pommel react?
      How will you communicate to the player what is Pullable and what is not, without
      breaking immersion? Think about the blue-line detection system from the books as
      a potential UI solution.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Is there a range on Ironpulling? What determines how far away a metal object
   can be before it becomes unreachable?

A: Yes, range exists. It scales with the Allomancer's power level and how much metal
   they are actively burning. Flaring iron extends reach. An average Lurcher can Pull
   metals within roughly a few dozen meters. Savants or those burning heavily can push
   this further. The books intentionally leave the exact number flexible.

GAME: Will range be a fixed stat, a scaling resource tied to metal reserves, or a
      player-upgradeable attribute? If flaring increases range at greater metal cost,
      how do you balance that economy? Consider whether range should be visible to the
      player as a radius indicator, or kept deliberately vague to encourage exploration
      and discovery. How does range interact with line-of-sight versus the blue-line
      detection system?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What governs which direction the PLAYER moves versus which direction the OBJECT
   moves when Pulling?

A: Relative mass. The lighter object moves more. Pull a coin lighter than you — the
   coin flies toward you. Pull a wall bracket bolted to a building (effectively infinite
   mass) — you fly toward it. This is consistent for both iron and steel. The physics
   follow Newton's third law throughout.

GAME: This is one of your most important physics design decisions. Will you simulate
      actual mass differentials, or use simplified rules (e.g. "anchored objects always
      pull the player, loose objects always come to the player")? How will the player
      learn this system intuitively without a tutorial wall of text? Consider whether
      environmental puzzles or combat scenarios can teach this organically. How will you
      handle edge cases like very heavy but unanchored objects?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does an iron metalmind store in Feruchemy, and what does it feel like to
   fill one versus tap one?

A: An ironmind stores weight. Filling it makes the Feruchemist literally lighter —
   easier to move, harder to stay grounded, almost ghostlike. Tapping makes them
   dramatically heavier — useful for anchoring themselves, breaking through floors,
   or becoming immovable in a grapple. Unlike Allomantic iron which affects external
   metals, Feruchemical iron is entirely about the user's own physical mass.

GAME: How will weight changes affect your physics and movement systems? A lighter
      player might be pushed by wind, float further when jumping, or struggle to resist
      knockback. A heavier player might break through weak floors, resist crowd control,
      or deal more damage in a charge. Consider whether weight storage has a visual
      tell — does the character visibly shrink or swell? How does this interact with
      swimming, climbing, or mounted movement if those exist in your game?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does an iron Hemalurgic spike steal, and from what part of the body must
   it be driven?

A: An iron spike steals Allomantic strength in Physical metals — specifically the
   powers of Ironpulling and Steelpushing. The spike must be driven through the
   bicep of the donor to correctly steal this power. Placement is critical — the
   wrong location steals a different or diminished power.

GAME: Will Hemalurgy be a player-accessible system or only an enemy/NPC mechanic?
      If players can use Hemalurgy, how will you handle the morality and consequence
      systems around spiking other characters? If it is enemy-only, consider how
      spiked enemies (koloss, Inquisitors, kandra) will have different power profiles
      based on their spikes. Will spike placement be simulated, or abstracted to
      spike type only?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  STEEL  |  Allomancy: Steelpushing  |  Feruchemy: Speed  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Physical Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: How does a Coinshot use steel to "fly," given that steel only Pushes metals?

A: By Pushing downward against coins dropped on the ground — or any metal anchor
   below them — the equal-and-opposite reaction launches the Allomancer upward. To
   sustain movement they keep dropping coins and Pushing, or Push against metals
   embedded in buildings. It is not true flight — it is controlled ballistic movement
   using consistent physics.

GAME: Traversal is likely one of your game's most visible and feel-defining systems.
      Will the player have infinite coins or a limited economy that makes every Push
      a resource decision? Consider the difference between Vin's acrobatic style and
      a more grounded approach. How will you design urban environments specifically
      to reward steel-based traversal — metal beams, iron railings, lamp posts?
      What is the "feel goal" for Steelpushing movement — weighty and deliberate,
      or fast and fluid?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Is there a range on Steelpushing? Can you Push on metals you cannot see?

A: Range exists and scales with power. Crucially, you can sense metals through
   Allomantic blue lines even through walls and in darkness, as long as they are
   within range. You do not need direct line-of-sight. This is one of the most
   tactically important mechanics in combat.

GAME: The blue-line detection system is both a UI challenge and an opportunity. Will
      you render blue lines as an always-on overlay, a toggleable mode, or something
      the player activates when burning iron or steel? How will you handle visual
      clutter in a metal-rich environment like Luthadel — too many lines could be
      overwhelming. Consider whether blue line thickness (based on metal mass) can
      double as an environmental puzzle mechanic, helping players identify key objects.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why does burning steel show the Allomancer blue lines pointing toward metals?
   What is the in-world explanation?

A: The blue lines are the Allomancer's perceptual sense granted by burning steel or
   iron. Lines vary in thickness based on the mass of the metal. There is no hard
   physics explanation given in Era 1 — it is understood as part of how Preservation's
   power manifests through the Allomancer. It lets you map a room through walls.

GAME: This is your built-in radar/detection system and it has enormous gameplay
      potential. Consider designing specific puzzles where blue lines are the only
      information available — a dark room, a hidden door, an ambush. How will enemy
      Allomancers interact with the blue-line system? Can enemies also sense the
      player's metal through their own blue lines, creating a mutual detection dynamic?
      Think about what happens to the blue-line display during a coppercloud.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a steelmind store, and what is the tradeoff of filling one?

A: A steelmind stores physical speed. While filling it, the Feruchemist moves
   sluggishly — banking their own speed for later. When tapping, they can move at
   superhuman velocity. The cost is always paid upfront. A Steelrunner who stores
   heavily will be frustratingly slow for days to build a meaningful reserve.

GAME: Speed changes have profound implications for gameplay feel. Will slowed
      movement during storage be a meaningful penalty or a minor inconvenience?
      Consider whether the player can pre-fill metalminds before dangerous situations
      or whether filling happens in real time with real consequences. When tapping,
      should the world visually slow down (bullet-time aesthetic) or should the
      player simply move faster? How will sped-up movement interact with combat
      hit detection and collision?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Steel in Allomancy and Steel in Feruchemy both relate to speed and movement.
   What is the fundamental difference in HOW the power is accessed?

A: Allomantic steel uses external metals as anchors to create kinetic force —
   it manipulates physical objects through external physics. Feruchemical steel
   stores and retrieves the user's own biological speed. One is an external
   physics-based push; the other is an internal personal enhancement. Same metal,
   completely different magic.

GAME: If your game includes both Allomancy and Feruchemy as player options, how
      will you communicate this distinction clearly without a lecture? Consider
      whether a character who has both (a Twinborn) might feel qualitatively
      different to play than one with only one system. Think about whether the
      visual and audio design for each system should feel distinct — perhaps
      Allomancy is loud and explosive, while Feruchemy is internal and quiet.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  TIN  |  Allomancy: Enhanced Senses  |  Feruchemy: Sensory Acuity  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Senses
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Burning tin enhances all five senses — but what is the dangerous downside of
   having enhanced senses in the wrong environment?

A: Enhanced senses can be weaponized against the Tineye. A sudden loud noise becomes
   agonizing. A bright flash is blinding. Strong smells are overwhelming. Vin learns
   this the hard way early in the series. The power itself becomes the weakness —
   having enhanced senses in combat is a liability as much as an asset.

GAME: This is one of the most game-design-ready mechanics in the entire series.
      Consider enemies or environmental traps designed specifically to counter a
      tin-burning player — flashbang-style explosions, deafening bell towers, smoke
      bombs with strong chemical smells. Will enhanced senses give the player tangible
      UI benefits like extended enemy audio detection radius? Balance the reward of
      enhanced perception against the deliberate sensory-overload counterplay.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Can a Tineye selectively enhance only some senses, or does burning tin always
   affect all five equally?

A: By default all five senses are enhanced simultaneously. Skilled Tineyes can learn
   to focus on specific senses and dampen others through practice, but this requires
   significant training and is not the natural default.

GAME: Consider making selective sense control a skill tree unlock or a practiced
      ability that improves with use. Early game: burning tin always creates the
      full vulnerability. Late game: the player can choose to enhance only sight
      while keeping hearing at normal levels. This creates natural character
      progression tied directly to mastery of the magic system. How will you teach
      this to the player without explicitly saying "you will unlock this later"?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a tinmind store, and how does it differ mechanically from
   Allomantic tin?

A: A tinmind stores sensory acuity — but crucially, the Feruchemist can choose
   which specific sense to store in a given tinmind. Feruchemical tin does not
   enhance all senses at once unless multiple tinminds are tapped simultaneously.
   Sazed uses this constantly — storing sight while reading large print, then
   tapping it to read small script later. The Feruchemical version offers more
   precise control than the Allomantic version.

GAME: The granularity of Feruchemical tin offers interesting puzzle design space.
      A player might store their night vision during a bright day to later tap it
      in a dark cave. Consider environmental puzzles that require specific sensory
      combinations. A door with a barely audible pattern lock, a poison identifiable
      only by smell, a hidden passage visible only with enhanced sight. How will
      you make the player feel clever for managing their sensory reserves correctly?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What is a Tineye savant and why is it considered dangerous or damaging?

A: A savant has burned tin so intensively and consistently that their senses are
   permanently heightened even without burning. Sounds are always too loud, lights
   always too harsh. Savantism is presented as a corruption, not a blessing — the
   power cannot be turned off. It functions as a cautionary tale about overuse.

GAME: Will savantism be a mechanic in your game? It creates a compelling risk-reward
      design space — powerful players who over-rely on a single metal could develop
      permanent drawbacks alongside permanent bonuses. Consider whether savantism
      is a player choice, an unavoidable consequence of heavy use, or something
      players can actively seek out as a build path. How does permanent sensory
      enhancement change your game's difficulty if the player cannot turn it off?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  PEWTER  |  Allomancy: Physical Enhancement  |  Feruchemy: Strength  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Physical Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Burning pewter enhances physical strength — but what OTHER benefits does it
   grant beyond raw strength?

A: Pewter enhances strength, speed, endurance, balance, and pain tolerance
   simultaneously. It also dramatically speeds healing from injuries sustained
   while burning. A Pewterarm can run for hours, take wounds that would cripple
   a normal person, and stay functional through injuries that would be immediately
   incapacitating. It is a full physical augmentation, not just a strength buff.

GAME: Pewter as a "combat mode" has enormous design potential. Consider whether
      burning pewter changes your combat feel entirely — heavier hits, different
      animation states, altered movement weight. Will pewter-burning affect the
      player's damage threshold or use a stagger/stun resistance system? The
      pain tolerance aspect could translate into a mechanic where pewter-burning
      players ignore status effects like bleeding or slowing that would affect
      a non-burning character.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What happens when a Pewterarm runs out of pewter mid-fight or during
   extended use?

A: The "pewter drag" hits. The body suddenly deals with all accumulated damage
   and exertion that pewter was masking. Broken bones that were held together
   suddenly become fully broken. Exhaustion that was suppressed floods back at
   once. Running someone out of pewter is a deliberate combat strategy.

GAME: This is a high-stakes resource management mechanic with massive gameplay
      implications. Consider a visual and mechanical "crash" state that punishes
      players who over-commit to pewter burning — dramatically slowed movement,
      reduced damage, vulnerability window. Enemies in your game who know about
      pewter drag would specifically try to outlast or deplete a Pewterarm's
      reserves before closing in. How long is the crash state? Can it be
      mitigated with other metals or Feruchemical health?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a pewtermind store, and what does the Feruchemist physically look
   like while filling versus tapping?

A: A pewtermind stores physical strength. While filling, the Feruchemist visibly
   weakens — muscles shrink and they struggle with ordinary tasks. When tapping
   heavily, muscles visibly bulge and expand to inhuman size temporarily. The
   books describe Feruchemists tapping pewter heavily as appearing almost
   monstrous in stature.

GAME: This is a strong visual opportunity. Consider dynamic character model
      changes tied to pewtermind state — a visibly diminished weak-state model
      and an enlarged powerful-state model. If your game has NPC reactions,
      a monstrous-looking player character tapping pewter heavily could cause
      fear or panic in crowds. Think about how this visual transformation
      interacts with stealth systems — you cannot exactly sneak when you
      look like a giant.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Both Allomantic pewter and Feruchemical pewter make you physically stronger.
   What is the key philosophical difference in where that strength comes from?

A: Allomantic pewter draws on external power — Preservation's Investiture channeled
   through the metal. The Allomancer gains something from outside themselves.
   Feruchemical pewter draws only on what the user already possesses — you bank
   your own strength and retrieve it. Feruchemy is end-neutral: no net gain,
   just redistribution across time.

GAME: Consider whether this philosophical distinction should have a mechanical
      expression in your game. Perhaps Allomantic pewter feels "borrowed" —
      it can be blocked, dampened, or stolen by Hemalurgy or specific enemies.
      Feruchemical pewter is intrinsic — harder to counter because it is
      genuinely the player's own stored attribute. This asymmetry could create
      interesting build diversity and enemy design around which system to target.

  SECTION 2 — MENTAL METALS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  ZINC  |  Allomancy: Riot Emotions  |  Feruchemy: Mental Speed  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Mental Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What exactly does Rioting an emotion do — and what does it NOT do?

A: Rioting amplifies and inflames an emotion that already exists in the target.
   If someone already feels a flicker of fear, a Rioter can make that fear
   overwhelming. Crucially it cannot create emotions from nothing. You cannot make
   someone hate someone they feel nothing about. You cannot instill loyalty where
   none exists. It is amplification, not creation.

GAME: Rioting as a player ability in a digital game raises fascinating design
      questions. Will NPCs have tracked emotional states that the player can
      read and amplify? Consider an emotion system where NPCs have baseline
      attitudes and the player can push those attitudes to extremes — a nervous
      guard becomes panicked and abandons their post, a suspicious merchant
      becomes paranoid and locks down. What prevents this from being a
      solve-everything button? The "cannot create from nothing" rule is your
      natural balancing mechanism.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Can a Rioter target a specific individual in a crowd, or does the effect
   hit everyone nearby?

A: Both are possible. Unfocused Rioting affects everyone in range. Focused Rioting
   requires concentration and can target a single person's specific emotion. Skilled
   operators like Breeze can surgically manipulate one person in a crowd without
   anyone else noticing. This precision makes skilled Rioters far more dangerous
   than unskilled ones.

GAME: Focused versus unfocused Rioting could function as two distinct input modes —
      a quick-use area effect and a slower precision interaction. Consider stealth
      implications: unfocused Rioting might alert nearby NPCs who notice others
      acting strangely, while focused Rioting leaves no visible trace. Skill
      progression could unlock finer precision — early game you riot a room,
      late game you riot exactly one guard's fear without their partner noticing.
      How will you represent the concentration cost of focused use?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a zincmind store, and how might filling one feel?

A: A zincmind stores mental speed — the quickness of thought. Filling makes you
   noticeably mentally slower, foggy, harder to focus. Tapping lets you think
   extraordinarily fast — processing complex scenarios in seconds, following
   multiple reasoning chains simultaneously. It does not increase raw intelligence,
   only the speed at which existing intelligence operates.

GAME: Mental speed is tricky to represent in a video game without resorting to
      bullet-time. Consider whether tapping a zincmind slows the world around
      the player for a few seconds, giving them time to plan a move in combat or
      a solution in a puzzle. While filling, you might introduce a deliberate
      UI fog — slower menu navigation, delayed NPC dialogue comprehension, reduced
      map reading speed. How will you make the player actually feel mentally slow
      without making the experience frustrating?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a zinc Hemalurgic spike steal?

A: A zinc spike steals Allomantic powers of the mental class — specifically the
   ability to affect emotions, both Rioting and Soothing. This follows Hemalurgy's
   consistent pattern: each metal steals powers thematically related to that
   metal's domain.

GAME: If enemy types in your game carry Hemalurgic spikes, zinc-spiked enemies
      would be capable of emotional manipulation — perhaps fear-inducing enemies
      that make the player's character act erratically or have impaired controls
      for a duration. Consider whether the player can remove spikes from defeated
      enemies and use them, raising the moral complexity of the Hemalurgy system.
      What are the consequences of having a spike — does it come with Ruin's
      influence as a persistent debuff?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  BRASS  |  Allomancy: Soothe Emotions  |  Feruchemy: Body Heat  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Mental Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does Soothing do, and how is a skilled Soother more dangerous than
   an unskilled one?

A: Soothing dampens and suppresses emotions. An unskilled Soother makes people
   feel vaguely calm. A skilled Soother like Breeze can suppress all emotions
   except one — leaving a target with only a single feeling while everything
   else is stripped away. This precision makes Soothing potentially more
   manipulative than Rioting.

GAME: Soothing as a stealth or social tool has enormous potential. Consider
      using it to pacify aggressive enemies without combat — a guard's hostility
      stripped away so they simply let you pass. A skilled player could use
      precision Soothing to isolate loyalty in a target NPC, making them
      cooperative despite having no prior relationship. How will you balance
      Soothing against Rioting in terms of utility? Will there be NPCs with
      enough emotional complexity that choosing what to suppress is a meaningful
      decision rather than always stripping everything to zero?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Is there any protection against Soothing or Rioting?

A: Yes. Aluminum protects against emotional Allomancy when worn or carried.
   A Smoker's coppercloud blocks emotional Allomancy for all within it, though
   a sufficiently powerful operator can push through. Another Allomancer actively
   burning brass or zinc can notice and counteract an emotional effect in progress.

GAME: Protection mechanics create counterplay depth. Consider environmental
      storytelling through aluminum — wealthy or paranoid NPCs might wear
      aluminum jewelry precisely to resist emotional manipulation, signaling
      their awareness of Allomancy. The coppercloud as a protection zone
      creates interesting tactical scenarios: safe houses within copperclouds,
      emotional-manipulation-free zones. How will the player know when their
      Soothing or Rioting is being resisted?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a brassmind store, and what is the physical experience of filling one?

A: A brassmind stores body heat. While filling, the Feruchemist becomes physically
   cold — body temperature drops noticeably. When tapping, they generate unusual
   warmth from within. It is one of the more unusual Feruchemical attributes with
   limited obvious combat application but significant survival utility.

GAME: Consider whether your game has environmental temperature systems — cold
      weather, blizzards, frozen environments. A brassmind becomes highly valuable
      in those contexts. Alternatively, the heat output when tapping could have
      secondary effects — melting ice, warming allies nearby, potentially igniting
      flammable objects. While filling in cold environments, the player might
      take cold damage faster. This could create an interesting risk: filling
      a brassmind while in a warm environment to spend the cold penalty safely.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  COPPER  |  Allomancy: Coppercloud  |  Feruchemy: Memory  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Mental Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a Smoker's coppercloud actually do, and what are its exact limits?

A: Burning copper creates a sphere around the Smoker that hides all Allomantic
   activity within it from Seekers burning bronze. Anyone burning metals inside
   the cloud is undetectable. The limits: it only hides Allomancy, not the people
   themselves. It does not make you invisible, quiet, or undetectable by non-
   Allomantic means. A sufficiently powerful Seeker can in rare cases detect through it.

GAME: The coppercloud is a zone-control mechanic with stealth implications.
      Consider the coppercloud as a placeable stealth field in your game —
      within it, the player's Allomantic activity cannot be detected by
      bronze-burning enemies. Enemies with bronze detection might be a
      persistent threat that makes the player constantly aware of their
      Allomantic signature. A coppercloud zone as a safe house or preparation
      area before a mission creates natural gameplay structure. What does the
      edge of a coppercloud look like visually?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why was a Smoker so strategically essential to Kelsier's crew even though
   copper seems like a "boring" power?

A: Without a Smoker, every Allomancer in the crew is detectable by Seekers.
   The Ministry trained Seekers specifically to hunt unauthorized Allomancers.
   Operating in Luthadel without a Smoker meant constant risk of exposure and
   capture. Copper is unglamorous but is the difference between operating safely
   for months and being caught in days.

GAME: Consider how to make support roles feel valuable and interesting in your
      game rather than mandatory but boring. If the player can recruit crew
      members, a Smoker NPC could provide a persistent passive benefit that
      lets the player use Allomancy more freely. Without one, the player
      might need to limit Allomantic use or accept higher detection risk.
      How will you communicate the threat of Seeker detection in a way
      that makes the player appreciate what a Smoker does for them?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a coppermind store, and why is it especially significant
   to Terris Keepers?

A: A coppermind stores memories — perfectly and completely. Anything stored is
   removed from the Feruchemist's mind while stored. Tapping recalls it perfectly,
   as if re-experiencing it. For Keepers like Sazed, copperminds are the foundation
   of their role: storing entire libraries of knowledge and cultural traditions the
   Lord Ruler tried to erase. Sazed's many copperminds are his life's work.

GAME: Memory storage has profound narrative potential in a video game. Consider
      a mechanic where the player can store memories of discovered locations,
      NPC conversations, or lore documents in a coppermind — removing them from
      active recall but allowing perfect retrieval later. This could serve as
      both a gameplay mechanic and an in-world explanation for a journal or
      codex system. Narratively, could a coppermind be stolen, recovered,
      or found containing someone else's memories as a story device?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Can one Feruchemist access another Feruchemist's coppermind?

A: No. This is a fundamental rule of Feruchemy. A metalmind can only be accessed
   by the Feruchemist who charged it. Another person touching it would sense power
   just out of reach but cannot use it. Sazed's copperminds are useless to anyone
   who kills him — the knowledge dies with his ability to tap them.

GAME: This rule has significant implications for your world design and quest
      structure. A dead Feruchemist's metalminds are effectively locked vaults —
      valuable but inaccessible. Consider quests built around finding ways to
      access lost metalminds, or the tragedy of a Keeper's knowledge being
      irretrievably lost. As a player protection mechanic, this means stolen
      metalminds cannot be used against the player by enemies. Does this
      rule hold even for Compounders or Hemalurgic spike interactions?
      Worth discussing with your AI.

================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  BRONZE  |  Allomancy: Detect Allomancy  |  Feruchemy: Wakefulness  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Mental Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What can a skilled Seeker detect when burning bronze, beyond just knowing
   that someone nearby is burning metals?

A: An unskilled Seeker only knows someone is burning something. A skilled Seeker
   can identify which specific metal is being burned — each metal produces a
   slightly different rhythm or pulse. Very skilled Seekers or savants can detect
   multiple Allomancers simultaneously and distinguish between them. Marsh
   demonstrates this ability throughout the series.

GAME: Seeker detection as an enemy ability creates a threat that punishes
      aggressive Allomantic use. Consider bronze-burning enemies that can
      pinpoint which of the player's metals are active and call in appropriate
      counters — calling a steel-armed soldier to handle a Coinshot, or deploying
      emotional dampeners against a Rioter. The detection skill gap between
      common Seekers and elite Seekers lets you design tiered enemy types.
      How will you communicate to the player that they have been detected,
      and by what?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What is the one thing that blocks a Seeker's bronze from working?

A: A Smoker burning copper. The coppercloud blinds bronze detection for all
   Allomancers within it. A Seeker outside the cloud cannot detect anyone
   burning metals inside. This copper-bronze counter-relationship is one of
   the most important tactical dynamics in the series, and why the Ministry
   hunted Smokers aggressively.

GAME: This creates a rock-paper-scissors dynamic worth formalizing in your
      design: Bronze detects Allomancy, Copper blocks detection, and powerful
      Seekers can sometimes pierce Copper. Consider making this visible in
      your enemy faction design — Ministry patrols might include both a Seeker
      and a soldier specifically assigned to find and kill Smokers first.
      How will the player learn that staying inside a coppercloud protects them?
      Can the player visually see when they are inside versus outside a cloud?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a bronzemind store, and how is it different from Allomantic bronze?

A: A bronzemind stores wakefulness — the body's need for sleep. Filling it
   makes the Feruchemist drowsy. Tapping lets them stay awake and alert far
   beyond normal human limits. The contrast with Allomantic bronze is total:
   the Allomantic version detects external Allomancy, while the Feruchemical
   version has nothing to do with detection — it is purely about biological
   need for rest.

GAME: Wakefulness could interact with a fatigue or rest system if your game
      has one. A player tapping bronzemind might be immune to sleep-inducing
      effects or environmental fatigue. Consider a stealth scenario where
      guards fall asleep at night but a player with tapped bronze can remain
      alert for 72 hours straight before crashing. Filling a bronzemind at
      an inopportune moment — becoming drowsy during an infiltration — could
      be a compelling risk-reward decision. Does your game have a day-night
      cycle that makes wakefulness mechanically relevant?

  SECTION 3 — HIGHER METALS

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  GOLD  |  Allomancy: Past Self Vision  |  Feruchemy: Health  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Feruchemical Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does burning gold actually show — and why is it considered
   psychologically dangerous?

A: Burning gold shows a vision of who you could have been — alternate versions
   of yourself based on different life choices. The experience is deeply
   unsettling because you see these ghost-versions of yourself alongside the
   real you. Most people find it profoundly disturbing. Vin's experience with
   gold is one of the trilogy's most philosophically charged moments.

GAME: This is a rare Allomantic power with narrative rather than combat utility.
      Consider using gold visions as a storytelling device — a way to show the
      player alternate backstories, reveal character history, or present moral
      dilemmas about choices already made. Could the player burn gold at key
      story moments to see "the road not taken"? Are these visions mechanically
      useful (revealing information) or purely atmospheric? The psychological
      cost could be a temporary mental state debuff representing the distress
      of confronting alternate selves.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does a goldmind store, and why is it one of the most practically
   desirable Feruchemical powers?

A: A goldmind stores health and healing ability. Filling makes the Feruchemist
   sickly and slow to heal. Tapping allows dramatically accelerated healing —
   recovering from wounds in hours that would take weeks. Broken bones knit,
   deep cuts close. In a world without advanced medicine, this is extraordinarily
   valuable. Wayne in Era 2 is effectively unkillable in combat because of his
   goldmind reserves.

GAME: The goldmind is your Feruchemical sustain system. Consider it as an
      alternative to traditional health potions — the player stores health
      during safe periods and taps it during emergencies. This creates
      pre-planning gameplay: you cannot just find a health item in a chest;
      you must spend time weakened to build reserves. Will healing be
      instant-use or gradual regeneration when tapping? How will you balance
      goldmind sustain against making the player feel invincible if they
      stored large reserves before a boss fight?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  ELECTRUM  |  Allomancy: Own Future Shadow
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Electrum is the "poor man's atium" — what does it actually show, and how
   is it different from atium?

A: Electrum shows the burner a shadow of their own future actions — a brief
   flickering glimpse of what they themselves are about to do. Atium shows the
   future actions of others. Electrum counters atium because if your opponent
   can see your future moves but you are also seeing your own future moves,
   you can consciously change them — making the atium vision inaccurate and
   the advantage meaningless.

GAME: Electrum creates a fascinating "nullification" mechanic. Consider whether
      burning electrum gives the player brief flickers of their own near-future —
      a ghost of where they will be a second from now. Against atium-burning
      enemies this becomes your counter tool. In design terms, electrum might
      manifest as a brief "phantom self" preview that the player can consciously
      deviate from to break an enemy Allomancer's prediction. How do you make
      the intentional act of "acting against your predicted future" feel
      satisfying and learnable?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why was the discovery of electrum so strategically important for the crew?

A: It effectively neutralized the Lord Ruler's greatest combat advantage —
   his seemingly limitless atium supply made him near-invincible. The crew
   realized burning electrum while fighting an atium-burner cancels the
   predictive advantage. Vin uses this in the climax of the first book.
   The solution to an "unbeatable" power was another metal that made
   the prediction useless.

GAME: This is an important design lesson embedded in the source material:
      every powerful mechanic should have a discoverable counter. Consider
      structuring your game so the player encounters atium-enhanced enemies
      that feel genuinely unbeatable before they discover electrum — making
      that discovery feel earned. How will you communicate to the player
      that electrum is the counter without simply telling them? Environmental
      storytelling, NPC hints, or a failed combat encounter that forces
      experimentation?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  ATIUM  |  Allomancy: See Others' Futures  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Feruchemical Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What exactly does burning atium show, and what are the physical and mental
   demands of using it in combat?

A: Burning atium shows ghostly images of what every nearby person and moving
   object is about to do — showing the near future. In combat you see attacks
   before they happen. The mental demand is severe: processing future-images of
   multiple opponents is cognitively overwhelming. Atium also burns extremely fast,
   making it prohibitively expensive to use for more than a few seconds.

GAME: Atium as a player ability is among your highest-risk design decisions.
      If the player can use it, it must feel extraordinary but limited. Consider
      a very short duration, a very limited resource pool, and a distinct visual
      state — ghost images of enemies' future movements rendered clearly.
      If atium is only an enemy or boss ability, it needs a readable tell so
      players understand why they cannot land hits. The cognitive overwhelm
      from multiple opponents could translate into the ghost images becoming
      cluttered and unreadable above a certain number of enemies — a built-in
      counter to atium being used in crowd scenarios.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Is an atium-burning Allomancer truly unbeatable? What are the counters?

A: Not unbeatable. Counters include: burning electrum (your own future shadow
   confuses atium's prediction), attacking with multiple opponents simultaneously
   so the cognitive load becomes unmanageable, simply waiting them out since
   atium burns incredibly fast and is rare, and acting with true randomness or
   pure instinct which partially defeats the prediction.

GAME: Designing counterplay into your most powerful ability is essential.
      Consider building each of these counters into your enemy AI and
      encounter design: swarm enemies specifically counter atium, while
      elite single enemies are countered by it. The "randomness defeats
      prediction" rule is interesting in a video game context — could
      certain attack patterns be flagged as unpredictable enough to
      partially bypass atium vision? Make sure the player discovers
      these counters through play before they face an atium boss.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What is atium at a cosmere level, and why was the Lord Ruler so desperate
   to control its supply?

A: Atium is the physical body of the Shard Ruin — condensed divine power in
   metal form. The Lord Ruler controlled the Pits of Hathsin, the only known
   source, and used atium as the empire's economic foundation. Controlling
   atium meant controlling wealth, power, and military supremacy simultaneously.
   Every bead burned was literally consuming Ruin's power — which is why Ruin
   wanted it hoarded, not burned.

GAME: The cosmere lore of atium has world-building implications for your game's
      economy and faction design. Consider whether atium scarcity is reflected
      in your economy — is it a currency, a crafting material, a plot-critical
      resource? The revelation that burning atium literally consumes a god's
      body could be a late-game narrative revelation with mechanical weight.
      How does atium's divine nature interact with your Hemalurgy system?
      Are there NPCs or factions aware of what atium truly is?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  ALUMINUM  |  Allomancy: Wipe Own Metals  |  Feruchemy: Identity  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Identity
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What happens when an Allomancer burns aluminum, and why is this considered
   "useless" on the surface?

A: Burning aluminum instantly destroys all other metals in the Allomancer's stomach
   — leaving them with nothing to burn. It grants no power; it removes the possibility
   of powers. On the surface this seems worthless. Strategically however: it protects
   against a Nicroburst forcing you to expend all your metals, and aluminum objects
   or clothing block external emotional Allomancy. Aluminum is an Allomantic null zone.

GAME: Consider aluminum as an emergency panic button — burning it when you are
      about to be Nicrob-burst by an enemy saves you from losing all your best
      reserves. Aluminum-lined armor or equipment as craftable items that reduce
      emotional Allomancy damage from enemies adds strategic depth to gear choices.
      The counterintuitive design of "a power that removes powers" is worth leaning
      into — it rewards players who understand the system deeply enough to know
      when destroying your own resources is the correct move.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does burning duralumin do, and what is the dramatic cost?

A: Burning duralumin while burning another metal creates a massive instantaneous
   burst of that metal's power — consuming both the duralumin and all remaining
   reserves of that metal in one explosive release. A Coinshot gets one Push
   that could hurl coins through walls. The cost is total: all metal is gone
   afterward. It is a one-shot nuclear option — incredibly powerful, leaves you
   completely defenseless after.

GAME: Duralumin is your "ultimate ability" mechanic and needs careful balancing.
      Consider it as a high-commitment finisher — the player must have built up
      reserves of another metal to make it worthwhile. The decision of which metal
      to nova with duralumin should feel meaningful and situational: nova pewter
      for a survival clutch, nova steel for an impossible-distance traversal move,
      nova zinc for a crowd-wide emotional break. The vulnerability window after
      use should be long enough to create real risk. How will you communicate
      to the player that they are now completely out of metals and exposed?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does an aluminum Hemalurgic spike steal, and why is it particularly
   disturbing?

A: An aluminum spike steals the target's Identity — their sense of self, their
   spiritual signature. A person stripped of Identity is a blank slate, their soul
   partially erased. This is what makes kandra so malleable with the right spikes.
   Hemalurgy stealing not just powers but personhood itself is one of the series'
   darkest revelations.

GAME: Identity theft through Hemalurgy opens dark narrative territory. Consider
      whether Identity-stripped characters in your game have visible behavioral
      tells — hollow responses, empty emotional reactions, susceptibility to
      commands. Kandra as a character type in your game would require a robust
      identity system to represent their ability to take on other personas.
      From a design perspective: if the player can have their Identity spiked
      or threatened, how does that manifest mechanically? Loss of skill memory?
      Altered faction relationships? Discuss the implications carefully with
      your AI before implementing.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  CHROMIUM / NICROSIL  |  Allomancy: Drain / Burst Others' Metals  |  Hemalurgy: Steal Enhancement Powers
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does burning chromium do, and how is it different from burning aluminum?

A: Chromium drains the metal reserves of someone you are touching — burning them
   away uselessly. Aluminum drains your own metals. Nicrosil bursts someone else's
   metals in a massive surge — forcing them to expend all reserves of one metal
   immediately. Both require physical contact, making them high-risk powers with
   extraordinary payoff against other Allomancers. These are anti-Allomancer weapons.

GAME: Contact-requirement powers create a close-range high-risk-high-reward archetype.
      Consider an enemy type built around chromium — a grappler that closes distance
      and attempts to drain the player's metal reserves, forcing the player to
      escape the grapple before their pewter or steel is gone. A player using
      chromium against enemies would need to close to melee range deliberately —
      a risky commitment for a devastating payoff. How will you handle the tactile
      feel of the drain effect? A visual of metals dissolving from the player's
      reserve UI would communicate it clearly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  CADMIUM / BENDALLOY  |  Allomancy: Slow / Speed Time Bubbles  |  Feruchemy: Breath / Nutrition
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What do cadmium and bendalloy do, and what is the key constraint on both powers?

A: Cadmium slows time in a bubble around the Allomancer. Bendalloy speeds time in
   a bubble. The crucial constraint: the Allomancer is always inside their own bubble.
   A cadmium-burner aging slowly is also stuck moving slowly relative to the world.
   A bendalloy-burner can act extremely quickly but the bubble is visible and
   stationary. Neither allows you to affect time for enemies without being in it yourself.

GAME: Time bubbles are one of your most visually spectacular potential mechanics.
      Bendalloy as a combat ability could let the player act several times within
      a single enemy action window — a brief flurry of hits before the bubble
      drops. Cadmium has more defensive and environmental use: slow down incoming
      projectiles, create a safe window to plan, outlast a timed mechanism.
      The "stationary bubble" constraint means the player cannot move and maintain
      the effect — forcing them to commit to a position. How will you visually
      represent the boundary of a time bubble and the distortion at its edge?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What are the physical effects on objects or people crossing the boundary
   of a time bubble?

A: Crossing the edge of a time bubble causes deflection — projectiles and objects
   passing through the boundary are deflected at an angle rather than continuing
   straight. This makes shooting into or out of a time bubble unreliable. The
   bubble's edge is also somewhat detectable because of this effect.

GAME: Deflection at the time bubble boundary is a built-in puzzle mechanic.
      Consider puzzles where the player must position a bendalloy bubble to
      deflect a projectile around a corner, or use cadmium deflection to
      redirect enemy fire back toward them. In combat, enemies firing into
      a bendalloy bubble will find their shots going wide — making the bubble
      both a speed boost and a partial projectile shield. Will the deflection
      angle be physically simulated or use a simpler rule-based redirect?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What do cadmium and bendalloy store in Feruchemy, and how do they contrast
   with their Allomantic versions?

A: A cadmiummind stores breath — the body's oxygenation needs. Tapping lets you
   go without breathing for extended periods. A bendalloy metalmind stores nutrition
   and calories. Filling while eating heavily, tapping when you need to survive
   without food. Both are completely different from their time-bubble Allomantic
   counterparts — Feruchemical cadmium and bendalloy are purely biological
   and metabolic.

GAME: Breath storage has clear survival mechanic applications — underwater sections,
      toxic gas environments, vacuum scenarios if your game has them. Consider
      a cadmiummind as equipment that allows extended underwater exploration
      or navigation through smoke-filled buildings without suffocation damage.
      Bendalloy nutrition storage is more niche but could matter in survival
      or resource-management game segments. Both could be implemented as
      passive equipment bonuses rather than active powers to avoid UI complexity.

  SECTION 4 — HEMALURGY SYSTEM-LEVEL QUESTIONS
================================================================================

Q: What are the two things that determine what a Hemalurgic spike steals —
   metal type AND what else?

A: Both the type of metal and the precise location on the body where the spike
   is driven. Metal type determines the category of power stolen. Placement
   determines the specific power within that category. The wrong location steals
   a different power, or steals it imperfectly. This knowledge was deliberately
   suppressed by the Lord Ruler — only he and the Steel Inquisitors had the
   complete map.

GAME: Consider whether spike placement is simulated in your game or abstracted.
      A full simulation opens interesting crafting depth — players experimenting
      with placement to find the right combination, NPCs or documents in the world
      containing fragments of the placement map as discoverable lore. An abstracted
      system (spike type determines power only) is simpler but loses some of the
      horror and precision of canon Hemalurgy. What level of complexity serves
      your game's tone and target player?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Hemalurgy steals powers — but what is always lost in the transfer, and
   why does this matter?

A: Hemalurgy is end-negative. Power is always lost in the transfer. The recipient
   always receives less than the donor had. This is why Inquisitors needed many
   spikes — each grants less than the original holder possessed. You cannot build
   an infinite power chain. There is always a cost, always entropy. Ruin's magic
   system literally embodies degradation.

GAME: End-negativity as a systemic property should be reflected in your mechanics.
      If players can use Hemalurgy, the math should always show diminishment —
      taking a power from an NPC gives a lesser version of what that NPC had.
      This prevents Hemalurgy from becoming an overpowered acquisition loop.
      It also creates a philosophical design statement: the game punishes
      Hemalurgic accumulation with diminishing returns, making it feel
      genuinely costly rather than just a different upgrade path.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: Why do Hemalurgic spikes create a "hole" in a person's soul, and how
   does Ruin exploit this?

A: The spike tears through the Spiritweb — the spiritual component of a person —
   creating a gap. This gap gives Ruin a point of access to influence and control
   the host's mind. This is how the Lord Ruler controlled kandra and koloss —
   through spikes that kept them permanently vulnerable to Ruin's whispers.
   Steel Inquisitors were similarly controllable. Power came at the price of autonomy.

GAME: Spiritual vulnerability as a mechanic has narrative and gameplay implications.
      Consider a Corruption or Influence system where spiked characters — including
      the player if they use Hemalurgy — accumulate Ruin's influence over time.
      At low levels this might be subtle whispers, audio cues, or strange impulses
      in the interface. At high levels it could begin affecting player control —
      brief moments of loss of agency, NPCs reacting to the player differently.
      This creates a genuine cost for Hemalurgic power that goes beyond resource loss.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: How many spikes does a Steel Inquisitor typically have, and what powers
   do those spikes give them?

A: Inquisitors have approximately ten spikes, most visibly through their eyes.
   Their spikes typically grant Allomantic steel and iron via the eye-spikes,
   plus combinations of other Allomantic and Feruchemical powers that vary by
   individual. The eye-spikes replace their eyes — they see via Allomantic metal
   detection rather than normal vision. Blinding them does not work.

GAME: Inquisitors as boss enemies are one of your most important design challenges.
      Their Allomantic metal-detection sight means darkness or blindness effects
      do not work against them — the player cannot simply turn off the lights.
      Their multiple spiked powers mean they should feel like they are multiple
      Allomancers in one body. Consider which spikes each Inquisitor has as a
      design choice that creates varied boss encounters — one Inquisitor heavy
      on pewter and steel, another on zinc and emotional manipulation. How will
      you communicate which powers an Inquisitor has before the fight begins?

  SECTION 5 — CROSS-SYSTEM QUESTIONS
================================================================================

Q: The three Metallic Arts are linked to the Shards Preservation and Ruin.
   Which system connects to which Shard, and what does that tell us about
   each system's nature?

A: Allomancy draws on Preservation — it is end-positive, gaining external power.
   Hemalurgy serves Ruin — it is end-negative, always losing power, always tearing.
   Feruchemy is balanced between both — end-neutral, neither gaining nor losing,
   only redistributing across time. This maps perfectly onto the Shards' philosophies:
   Preservation creates and sustains, Ruin destroys and degrades, Feruchemy
   represents the equilibrium that existed before their conflict.

GAME: This three-system philosophical framework could be your design's thematic
      backbone. Consider whether your game's tone and narrative reinforce these
      associations — Allomancy as explosive and external, Hemalurgy as dark and
      costly, Feruchemy as balanced and internal. If the player can use all three,
      think about whether a "corruption" or "alignment" system tracks which magic
      they use most heavily and reflects that in story outcomes or NPC reactions.
      What does a player who relies entirely on Hemalurgy tell you about their
      playstyle — and should the game acknowledge that?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: What does "Compounding" mean, and why is it so extraordinarily powerful?

A: Compounding is when a Twinborn uses the same metal for both Allomancy and
   Feruchemy. They store an attribute Feruchemically, then burn the charged
   metalmind Allomantically — which supercharges the Feruchemical output to
   enormous levels. The Lord Ruler compounded gold: stored health, then burned
   the goldmind Allomantically to get near-infinite regeneration. Compounding
   converts external Investiture into amplification of stored personal attributes —
   turning end-neutral Feruchemy into something end-positive.

GAME: Compounding is your late-game power fantasy system and requires very careful
      balancing. Consider gating it behind significant progression — the player
      must understand both magic systems deeply before they can exploit their
      combination. The Lord Ruler's near-immortality via gold Compounding suggests
      that unrestricted Compounding breaks the game. Think about whether Compounding
      is a player ability at all, or reserved for specific story-significant characters.
      If it is available, what is the cost or limitation that prevents it from
      trivializing all combat? Discuss this one carefully with your AI — it is
      the most balance-sensitive mechanic in the system.
